A 2-D lattice-Boltzmann (D2Q9 BGK) simulation of the disc's centre cross-section, computed in the browser. Stagnation, separation, and vortex shedding behave qualitatively as they do in flight, but the section is 2-D and runs near Re 10³ where flight is near 10⁵, so treat the picture as illustrative. The flight numbers used elsewhere on this site come from the 3-D steady-RANS sweep below.

Reading this page

The disc is not spinning, here or in the RANS sweep. At flight Reynolds numbers spin has little effect on time-averaged lift, drag, and pitching moment. Its main role is gyroscopic: it holds the disc's attitude and converts aerodynamic pitch torques into slow roll precession instead of a tumble. Rotating (MRF) RANS runs are the planned next step.

Flight paths in the designer do not come from this tunnel. The 300 solved geometries below fit a polynomial surrogate giving Cl, Cd, and Cm as functions of shape and angle of attack, and a 6-DOF integrator adds gravity, spin, and precession. This page shows the flow those coefficients summarize.

Angle of attack drives most of what you see. At low α the flow stays attached and the wake is thin. As α rises the upper surface separates earlier, the wake widens, and the centre of pressure moves forward; past roughly 10–15° the section stalls. Geometry shifts these transitions: dome adds camber, a lower parting line moves the centre of pressure aft, and the nose radius sets where the flow separates.

The black arrow is the net aerodynamic force measured from the simulation; the orange dot is the centre of pressure. A CoP ahead of the disc's centre applies a nose-up torque, which spin precesses into rightward roll on a RHBH throw: turn. A CoP behind centre gives a nose-down torque and leftward roll: fade. The Cm(α) slope in the charts below is the same quantity measured in 3-D, which is why parting-line height is the designer's main stability knob.
